Mastering Roguelike Game Design: From Permadeath to Procedural Generation

Chapter 9.2: Implementing Multiple Ending Scenarios

Multiple endings are a powerful tool in roguelike game design, offering players varied goals, replayability, and a sense of agency. This chapter explores strategies for implementing diverse ending scenarios that enhance the roguelike experience.

The Role of Multiple Endings in Roguelikes

In roguelikes, multiple endings serve several purposes:

  1. Replayability: Encourage players to explore different strategies and paths.
  2. Narrative Depth: Expand the game's story and lore through varied outcomes.
  3. Player Agency: Reward different playstyles and decision-making.
  4. Difficulty Scaling: Offer more challenging paths for experienced players.

Types of Ending Scenarios

1. Victory Variations

  • Standard Victory: Completing the main objective (e.g., defeating the final boss).
  • Perfect Victory: Achieving specific additional criteria (e.g., no deaths, collecting all artifacts).
  • Speed Run: Completing the game within a time limit.

2. Character-based Endings

  • Class-specific Endings: Unique conclusions based on the chosen character class.
  • Alignment Endings: Outcomes determined by moral choices made during the run.
  • Relationship Endings: Conclusions affected by interactions with NPCs or factions.

3. Quest-driven Endings

  • Main Quest Variations: Different outcomes based on how the primary quest is resolved.
  • Side Quest Impact: Endings influenced by the completion of optional quests.
  • Hidden Quests: Secret paths leading to unique endings for observant players.

4. Resource-based Endings

  • Wealth Endings: Outcomes determined by the amount of treasure or currency accumulated.
  • Knowledge Endings: Conclusions based on lore or secrets discovered.
  • Item-specific Endings: Special endings triggered by collecting or using particular items.

5. World State Endings

  • Environmental Impact: Endings reflecting how the player's actions affected the game world.
  • Faction Dominance: Conclusions based on which factions the player supported or defeated.
  • Corruption/Purity: Endings determined by the level of corruption or purity in the world or character.

Implementing Multiple Endings

1. Decision Tracking

  • Implement a system to track key decisions, achievements, and stats throughout the run.
  • Use flags or variables to mark significant events that could influence endings.

2. Ending Determination Logic

  • Create a hierarchy or scoring system to determine which ending is triggered.
  • Consider using a combination of factors rather than single-trigger conditions.

3. Narrative Integration

  • Weave hints about potential endings into the game's narrative and environment.
  • Use foreshadowing to make endings feel natural and earned.

4. Visual and Audio Cues

  • Implement subtle visual or audio changes to reflect the player's path towards specific endings.
  • Create unique assets (cutscenes, music, art) for each ending to make them memorable.

5. Ending Screens and Summaries

  • Design informative ending screens that recap the player's journey and choices.
  • Consider including stats, achievements, and "what-if" hints to encourage replays.

6. New Game+ Integration

  • Use completed endings to unlock new content or starting conditions in subsequent playthroughs.
  • Implement a system where certain endings unlock new character classes or game modes.

Balancing Multiple Endings

1. Accessibility vs. Secrecy

  • Ensure some endings are relatively easy to achieve for casual players.
  • Hide more complex or "true" endings behind challenging requirements for dedicated players.

2. Telemetry and Playtesting

  • Use player data to track which endings are rarely seen or too easily achieved.
  • Adjust requirements based on player feedback and completion rates.

3. Narrative Satisfaction

  • Ensure each ending provides a satisfying conclusion, even if it's not the "best" ending.
  • Balance the narrative weight of different endings to make various paths feel worthwhile.

Conclusion

Implementing multiple ending scenarios in roguelikes adds depth, replayability, and player agency to the game. By carefully designing a variety of endings tied to different aspects of gameplay, developers can create a rich, rewarding experience that encourages exploration and mastery of the game's systems. Remember, the goal is not just to have many endings, but to have meaningful conclusions that reflect the player's journey and choices throughout their roguelike adventure.
