Mastering Roguelike Game Design: From Permadeath to Procedural Generation

10.3 Strategy Games with Roguelike Features

The integration of roguelike elements into strategy games has created a compelling subgenre that combines the depth of strategic decision-making with the unpredictability and replayability of roguelikes. This chapter explores how roguelike features enhance strategy gameplay, the challenges of this integration, and examples of successful implementations.

Roguelike Elements in Strategy Games

  1. Procedural Generation

    • Randomized maps
    • Variable starting conditions
    • Dynamic event systems

  2. Permadeath (or High-stakes Consequences)

    • Permanent unit loss
    • Campaign-ending failure conditions

  3. Run-based Gameplay

    • Self-contained campaigns or scenarios
    • Emphasis on replayability and adaptation

  4. Incremental Unlocks and Meta-progression

    • New units, abilities, or starting conditions unlocked over multiple playthroughs
    • Persistent upgrades or modifications to game rules

  5. Randomized Elements

    • Unit availability
    • Resource distribution
    • Tech trees or upgrade paths

Enhancing Strategy with Roguelike Features

Procedural Generation in Strategy

  • Benefit: Ensures no two games are alike, forcing players to adapt strategies to new scenarios.
  • Implementation: Algorithms for balanced map generation, including resource distribution and strategic points of interest.
  • Example: "Civilization VI" uses procedural generation for its maps, creating unique geopolitical landscapes each game.

Permadeath and Consequences

  • Benefit: Raises the stakes of each decision, making the game more engaging and tense.
  • Implementation: Permanent unit loss or irreversible consequences for failed missions or poor resource management.
  • Example: "XCOM: Enemy Unknown" features permanent death for squad members, making each tactical decision crucial.

Run-based Campaigns

  • Benefit: Allows for tighter, more focused strategic experiences that encourage multiple playthroughs.
  • Implementation: Short, intense campaigns with clear win/lose conditions and high replayability.
  • Example: "FTL: Faster Than Light" offers compact space strategy runs with a clear end goal.

Meta-progression in Strategy Contexts

  • Benefit: Provides a sense of progression across multiple playthroughs, encouraging experimentation with new strategies.
  • Implementation: Unlockable units, technologies, or starting conditions that expand strategic options over time.
  • Example: "Slay the Spire" unlocks new cards and relics for future runs, expanding strategic depth.

Design Considerations for Strategy Roguelikes

  1. Balancing Randomness and Skill

    • Challenge: Ensuring that strategic skill remains the primary factor in success, despite random elements.
    • Solution: Carefully calibrate the impact of random events and provide players with tools to mitigate bad luck.

  2. Information Management

    • Challenge: Presenting complex, ever-changing strategic information clearly to the player.
    • Solution: Develop intuitive UI systems that highlight key information and help players track changing variables.

  3. Pacing and Session Length

    • Challenge: Balancing the depth of strategy games with the quicker pace often associated with roguelikes.
    • Solution: Design systems that allow for meaningful strategic decisions within shorter play sessions.

  4. Replayability vs. Narrative

    • Challenge: Incorporating compelling narrative elements into highly replayable, procedurally generated scenarios.
    • Solution: Develop modular narrative systems or focus on emergent storytelling through gameplay.

  5. Difficulty Curve and Learning

    • Challenge: Creating a game that's accessible to new players but maintains long-term challenge for veterans.
    • Solution: Implement progressive difficulty systems and offer varied challenges that test different strategic skills.

Case Studies

Into the Breach

"Into the Breach" by Subset Games exemplifies the successful integration of roguelike elements into a turn-based strategy game:

  • Procedural Generation: Each island and its missions are procedurally generated, offering new tactical puzzles each run.
  • Permadeath with a Twist: While individual units can be lost, the game's time-travel mechanic allows players to start a new timeline, retaining some progress.
  • Run-based Gameplay: Each campaign is compact and highly replayable, with clear win conditions.
  • Meta-progression: Players unlock new squads and pilots over multiple runs, expanding strategic options.
  • Perfect Information: Despite its roguelike elements, the game provides complete information about enemy actions, emphasizing strategic planning.

Wildermyth

"Wildermyth" blends procedural storytelling with tactical RPG elements:

  • Procedural Narrative: Each campaign generates unique characters and storylines, creating emergent narratives.
  • Legacy System: Characters can become legacy heroes, returning in future campaigns with retained traits and relationships.
  • Tactical Combat: Turn-based battles on procedurally generated maps test players' strategic skills.
  • Permadeath: Characters can die permanently, but their legends live on through the legacy system.

Conclusion

The integration of roguelike elements into strategy games has opened up new avenues for replayability, emergent storytelling, and strategic depth. By carefully balancing randomness with skill-based gameplay, designers can create experiences that challenge players to adapt their strategies constantly. As this subgenre continues to evolve, we can expect to see even more innovative ways of blending the depth of strategy games with the unpredictability and excitement of roguelikes.
