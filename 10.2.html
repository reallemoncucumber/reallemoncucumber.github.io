Mastering Roguelike Game Design: From Permadeath to Procedural Generation

10.2 Implementing Roguelike Elements in Action Games

The fusion of roguelike elements with action game mechanics has led to some of the most innovative and engaging titles in recent years. This chapter explores how developers can effectively implement roguelike features in action games, creating experiences that blend the intensity of real-time combat with the unpredictability and replayability of roguelikes.

Core Roguelike Elements in Action Games

  1. Procedural Generation

    • Level layouts
    • Enemy placement
    • Item and power-up distribution

  2. Permadeath (or Modified Versions)

    • Full run resets
    • Partial progress loss

  3. Run-based Gameplay

    • Distinct, self-contained play sessions
    • Emphasis on replayability

  4. Randomized Loot and Upgrades

    • Varied character builds
    • Synergistic item combinations

  5. Progressive Difficulty

    • Scaling challenges
    • Risk-reward decisions

Adapting Roguelike Elements for Action Gameplay

Procedural Generation in Real-time Environments

  • Challenge: Ensuring generated levels are balanced and navigable in real-time.
  • Solution: Implement a modular design system with pre-designed room templates that can be procedurally assembled.
  • Example: "Dead Cells" uses a system of pre-designed rooms arranged procedurally, ensuring fluid movement and combat spaces.

Balancing Permadeath with Player Progression

  • Challenge: Maintaining long-term player engagement despite frequent setbacks.
  • Solution: Introduce meta-progression systems that persist between runs.
  • Example: "Hades" allows players to upgrade the protagonist's home base and unlock new weapons between runs, providing a sense of progress even after death.

Randomized Loot in Skill-based Combat

  • Challenge: Ensuring player skill remains central while incorporating random elements.
  • Solution: Design a core combat system that's engaging on its own, with randomized loot enhancing rather than defining the experience.
  • Example: "Enter the Gungeon" features tight, skill-based shooting mechanics, with randomized guns adding variety to each run.

Implementing Run Diversity

  • Challenge: Creating enough variety to keep each run feeling fresh.
  • Solution: Develop a wide pool of enemies, items, and environmental hazards that can be mixed and matched.
  • Example: "The Binding of Isaac" offers hundreds of items that can be combined in unique ways, dramatically altering each playthrough.

Design Considerations

  1. Pacing and Flow

    • Design levels to alternate between high-intensity combat and moments of exploration or decision-making.
    • Example: "Hades" intersperses combat rooms with story encounters and shop rooms.

  2. Learning Curve and Difficulty Scaling

    • Implement systems that allow players to gradually master the game's mechanics across multiple runs.
    • Consider adaptive difficulty systems that respond to player performance.

  3. Feedback and Information Clarity

    • Ensure players can quickly understand new items, enemies, and environmental effects in the heat of action.
    • Use clear visual and audio cues to convey important information.

  4. Synergies and Emergent Gameplay

    • Design items and abilities that can interact in interesting and unexpected ways.
    • Encourage players to experiment with different combinations.

  5. Persistence and Player Investment

    • Balance the roguelike element of starting fresh with some form of persistent progression or unlocks.
    • Consider incorporating narrative elements that evolve over multiple runs.

Case Study: "Returnal"

"Returnal" by Housemarque is an excellent example of roguelike elements implemented in a AAA action game:

  • Procedural Generation: Biomes are procedurally arranged while maintaining a consistent atmosphere and navigability.
  • Permadeath: Players restart from the beginning upon death, but certain key items and narrative progress persist.
  • Weapon Proficiency: A run-based progression system that encourages players to stick with and master weapons they find.
  • Risk-Reward: Parasites and malignant items offer powerful buffs with potentially hazardous drawbacks.
  • Narrative Integration: The story unfolds gradually across multiple runs, with persistent elements tying the experience together.

Conclusion

Implementing roguelike elements in action games requires a delicate balance between randomization and designed experiences. When done effectively, this combination can create highly replayable, engaging games that challenge players' skills while offering fresh experiences with each playthrough. As the genre continues to evolve, we can expect to see even more innovative ways of blending roguelike elements with action gameplay, pushing the boundaries of game design and player engagement.
